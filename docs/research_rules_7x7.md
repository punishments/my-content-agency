# 7x7 Research Rules

Hard rules and guardrails for the research system. These exist to keep research useful, honest, and connected to your actual brand — not a collection of interesting observations that never become content.

---

## What to Prioritize

**1. Shares over views.**
Views measure distribution. Shares measure resonance. Your brand is built on resonance. When two posts are competing for your attention, always study the one with the higher share rate first.

**2. Mechanism over topic.**
The topic of a video doesn't teach you anything reusable. The mechanism does. "This worked because it named an anxiety the audience carries privately" is useful. "This worked because it was about burnout" is not.

**3. Outliers over averages.**
Study the posts that broke through, not the posts that were consistent. Consistency tells you what a creator's floor is. Outliers tell you what's possible.

**4. Emotional specificity over broad categories.**
"It triggered emotion" is not an insight. "It surfaced the specific shame of feeling behind while watching everyone else figure it out" is. Push your analysis until it's that specific.

**5. Adaptation over observation.**
Every analysis entry must end with what you can apply to your own content. Research with no adaptation is entertainment, not work.

---

## What to Ignore

- **Post quality that isn't replicable** — if it went viral because of a celebrity mention, a news tie-in, or random algorithm luck, there's nothing to learn from the mechanism
- **Metrics without context** — a post with 1M views from a 500K-follower creator is not an outlier. A post with 200K views from a 5K-follower creator is.
- **Generic hooks that work because of raw distribution** — "5 tips for productivity" posts sometimes go viral via paid push or following. Don't extract those as signal.
- **Formats you cannot execute** — if a creator's format requires a studio, a large cast, or expensive production, note it, but don't add it to your format library as something to test.
- **Content that overlaps with your niche but serves a different audience** — family content, parenting humor, finance bros — interesting to observe, not useful to adapt.

---

## Anti-Copying Rules

**Never copy a hook directly.** Extract the pattern, then rewrite it with your own angle. "POV: woman in her 30s changing careers every month" → pattern: POV + identity marker + recurring absurd behavior → your version: "POV: person who has tabs open on three different career paths and a book on all of them."

**Never use the same topic.** If a creator made a video about burnout and it performed well, that's a signal about emotional resonance with that topic — but you need to bring your own angle, your own experience, your own frame. Burnout from stagnating in a job you outgrew is different from burnout from overwork. Find the specific.

**Never replicate the exact structure AND topic.** You can borrow a structure. You can touch a similar theme. But doing both is derivative and your audience will sense it, even if they can't name it.

**The test:** If someone saw your content next to the creator you studied, would they say "that's inspired by X"? If yes, you haven't adapted far enough.

---

## How to Avoid Generic Conclusions

**Reject any insight that could appear in a generic content creation article.** "Short hooks perform better." "Captions improve retention." These are table stakes, not insights.

**Ask "why does this work for this audience specifically?"** A no-payoff hook works for an audience that already feels unresolved about their lives. Your audience feels stuck, overthinking, and behind — which means withheld resolution activates them differently than it would activate, say, a travel content audience.

**Push one level deeper on every observation.** If your first answer is a category ("it used an identity hook"), your second answer should be a mechanism ("it named a private shame the audience hadn't said out loud"), and your third should be an application ("my version: the specific shame of being smart but not executing").

**Discard any "insight" you can't connect to a specific post.** If you can't point to the exact moment in the video where the mechanism activated, it's a hypothesis, not an observation.

## How to Adapt Findings to Your Brand and Audience

Before adding anything to your libraries, run it through this filter:

**1. Would my audience stop scrolling for this?**
Ambitious, introspective, 23–35. Overthinking. Career crossroads. Not drawn to hustle culture or performative positivity. Would this hook, format, or structure land for that person specifically?

**2. Can I make this mine through lived experience?**
The strongest adaptation is always rooted in something real. Don't use a pattern unless you have a real story, system, or observation to ground it in.

**3. Does this fit my voice?**
Grounded, honest, slightly analytical, emotionally aware, calm. If the pattern requires hype, shock, or performance that doesn't feel natural, note it but don't force it.

**4. Which pillar does this belong to?**
Map every adapted idea to Skill, Mindset, Lifestyle, or Passions before it goes into the backlog. If you can't place it, the adaptation isn't specific enough yet.

**5. What mission does it serve?**
Attract, Nurture, Position, or Convert. If you can't decide, it's usually trying to do too many things at once — narrow it.

## What Makes a Research Insight Actually Useful

A useful insight has all four of these:

1. **It's specific** — you can point to the exact mechanism, not just the category
2. **It's repeatable** — it appeared in multiple posts or multiple creators, not just once
3. **It's adaptable** — you can write a concrete "your version" for your brand and audience
4. **It's actionable** — it leads directly to a content idea you can script this week

An insight that fails any of these is an observation. Keep observations in your notes. Only proven, adapted, actionable patterns go into the libraries.

---

## Final Check Before Closing a Research Cycle

Before you consider a cycle complete:

- [ ] Every post has a "why it worked" that names a specific mechanism
- [ ] Every insight has a "how to apply it to my brand" section
- [ ] At least 3 new patterns have been added to the libraries (or existing ones updated)
- [ ] At least 5 content ideas have been added to the backlog
- [ ] Nothing in your libraries could be mistaken for generic content advice
