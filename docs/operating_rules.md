# Operating Rules

These rules govern every content decision. Non-negotiables first. Hard stops second.

---

## 5 Non-Negotiable Content Rules

### 1. Every piece must earn its place with a specific insight.
Not a general take. Not a restatement of what everyone already knows. One real, specific, non-obvious thing that only comes from lived experience or genuine research. If you can't name it, you're not ready to script it.

### 2. The audience must feel seen before they're taught anything.
Start with the experience — their frustration, their confusion, their stuck moment. Connection before instruction. If the content opens with advice before it opens with understanding, it fails.

### 3. One idea per piece. Always.
Short-form is not the place for everything you know on a topic. Pick the single most valuable angle. Cut everything that belongs in a different video. One idea, fully delivered, is more powerful than three ideas half-delivered.

### 4. The scripting prompt is mandatory. No workarounds.
Every script goes through the structured prompt in `docs/scripting_prompt.md`. No improvised scripts, no skipping inputs, no "it's a quick one" exceptions. The structure exists because it works.

### 5. If it can't be tracked, it doesn't count.
Every published piece gets an analytics file in `/analytics/`. Performance review happens at 48 hours and 7 days. The learning loop is part of the work — not optional.

---

## 5 "Never Do This" Rules

### 1. Never post content that could belong to any other creator.
Run every piece through the test: could this live on a generic self-help, productivity, or career account and fit perfectly? If yes — it needs a real angle, a specific story, or a hard cut. Generic is invisible.

### 2. Never use hype language or empty urgency.
No "game-changer," "life-transforming," "you NEED to know this," or forced scarcity. The audience is smart. Hype language signals that there's nothing real underneath. Let the idea carry the weight.

### 3. Never preach or moralize.
This brand does not tell people what they should do with their lives. It shares what's worked, what's failed, and what's real. The tone is peer-to-peer — someone who's in it too, not above it. The moment it gets preachy, it loses the audience.

### 4. Never sacrifice clarity for creativity.
An interesting concept that's confusing is worthless. A clever hook that doesn't connect to the content is a broken promise. Clarity wins every time. If someone has to rewatch to understand the point, the script failed.

### 5. Never skip the brand check.
An idea that feels exciting in the moment isn't automatically on-brand. Every idea gets checked against the creator vision before it enters the backlog. Time spent on off-brand content is time not spent building the brand.

---

## The One-Line Test

Before any piece of content goes into production, it should be able to answer this in one sentence:

> **"This content helps [specific audience] [feel/do/understand what] — and only this brand could have made it."**

If it can't pass that test, it's not ready.
