# Hook Library

Reusable hook patterns extracted from 7x7 research. Organized by type. Each entry includes the pattern, an example from the data, why it works, and how to apply it to your brand.

---

## 1. Identity / Anxiety Hooks

**What they do:** Name a specific fear, pressure, or internal state that the audience already carries. The hook doesn't create the feeling — it surfaces it. Viewer recognition is instant.

---

**Pattern: Societal Timeline Callout**
> "I'm running out of time I should settle down and have kids"
> "POV: GIRLIE IN HER 30S RUNNING OUT OF TIME"
> "I'm 30 years old and I'm freaking out"

Why it works: It names the exact voice inside their head. They feel caught — in a good way. The relief of "someone else said it" is immediate.

Adaptation for your brand:
> "I'm supposed to know what I'm doing by now"
> "POV: person in their late 20s who's been 'figuring it out' for 3 years"
> "At some point you stop calling it a career path and start calling it survival"

---

**Pattern: Anti-Resolution Hook**
> "just to be clear I'm probably not going to stop doomscrolling"
> "i've decided to spend this year actually trying (fuck)"
> "2026 is going to be my year / January 3rd:"

Why it works: Refuses the motivational format. The viewer who's been failed by motivation responds to someone who isn't pretending.

Adaptation:
> "I didn't fix my overthinking. I just got better at noticing it."
> "I still procrastinate. I just do it more efficiently now."
> "This isn't a 'I changed my life' story. It's an 'I'm still in it' story."

---

**Pattern: Internal Monologue Externalized**
> 🧠: "don't post your videos, no one will watch them"
> "I should travel before I have kids" / "but I need to save for a house" / "soon it will be too late"

Why it works: The brain emoji or POV frame gives the viewer permission to acknowledge a thought they've been ashamed of. The externalization is the relief.

Adaptation:
> 🧠: "you're too late to start"
> 🧠: "everyone's further ahead than you"
> 🧠: "if you were really good at this, you wouldn't need to try this hard"

## 2. POV Hooks

**What they do:** Put the viewer inside a specific moment. Not about you — about them. The POV is the lens, not the subject.

---

**Pattern: POV + Identity Marker + Moment**
> "POV: woman in her 30s changing careers every month"
> "POV: moment right before quitting the 9-5 that drained the life out of you"
> "POV: you're just a mom that wants a holiday"

Why it works: Three elements working together — role (who), state (where they are), specific moment (when). All three narrow the audience fast. The right person feels directly addressed.

Formula: `POV: [identity/role] + [recurring situation or moment]`

Adaptation:
> "POV: person who finally has a good idea but can't make themselves start"
> "POV: you've been 'almost ready' to post for three weeks"
> "POV: the first time someone actually pays you for what you're good at"
> "POV: you left the meeting and immediately thought of what you should have said"

---

**Pattern: POV + Emotional State (No Explanation)**
> "POV: moment right before quitting the 9-5 that drained the life out of you" (just the expression — 4 seconds)
> "i won't tell anyone my spark is back but there will be signs"

Why it works: No explanation needed. The viewer projects their own version of the moment. The less specific the visual, the more universal the feeling.

## 3. Contrarian Hooks

**What they do:** Challenge a widely held belief or expected behavior. Creates friction (good friction) that forces the viewer to keep watching to resolve their disagreement or curiosity.

---

**Pattern: Smart vs. Dumb Inversion**
> "there's something I noticed about smart people that makes them lose to dumb people"

Why it works: Calls out the target audience by their self-concept (smart), then threatens it. Viewer can't not watch.

Adaptation:
> "The people who think the most tend to act the least"
> "Overthinking isn't a sign of intelligence. It's a sign of fear dressed up as strategy."
> "The least qualified people I know have the most confidence. Here's what I took from that."

---

**Pattern: Prerequisite Flip**
> "do this before you make your vision board"
> "I made a decision about my life in 2025 that I believe has been the most impactful" → [the decision: releasing potential as a concept]

Why it works: Implies the thing everyone does (vision board, chasing potential) is missing a step. Creates urgency around the setup before a single word of value is delivered.

Adaptation:
> "do this before you update your resume"
> "do this before you try to build a habit"
> "do this before you ask for a raise"

---

**Pattern: Reframe the Accepted Pain**
> "competition doesn't exist when you only see your own potential"
> "the most expensive thing you can wear is:"

Why it works: Takes something the audience accepts as bad (comparison anxiety, lack of confidence) and reframes it as a choice, not a condition.

Adaptation:
> "The career you want isn't behind a better strategy. It's behind a clearer identity."
> "You're not behind. You're on a different timeline that no one else has run yet."
> "Execution isn't a discipline problem. It's an identity problem."

---

## 4. Curiosity / Open Loop Hooks

**What they do:** Create a question the viewer can't answer without finishing the video. The hook opens a gap. The content closes it.

---

**Pattern: Withheld Completion**
> "the most expensive thing you can wear is:"
> "i won't tell anyone my spark is back but there will be signs"
> "I made a decision about my life in 2025 that I believe has been the most impactful decision" [what was it?]

Why it works: Incomplete sentences or statements force the brain to complete the loop. The video becomes the answer key.

Adaptation:
> "The one thing that changed my communication more than anything else was:"
> "I stopped feeling behind the moment I realized:"
> "The thing that actually separates the people who execute from the ones who don't:"

---

**Pattern: Tension + No Option**
> "this is a very uncomfortable video to make right now but I don't have any other choice"
> "A lot of people seem to believe that the reason I'm able to speak quickly is because I have so many cuts" [and they're wrong]

Why it works: Declares stakes before the content begins. Viewer is already leaning in.

Adaptation:
> "I don't want to say this but I think it would help someone"
> "The thing I wish someone had told me three years ago"
> "This is not advice. This is something I had to learn the hard way."

---

**Pattern: Implied Transformation (No Payoff Shown)**
> "i won't tell anyone my spark is back but there will be signs"
> "i've decided to spend this year actually trying (fuck)"

Why it works: Announces a change without delivering the evidence. The viewer follows to see if it's true. No-payoff = highest follow incentive.

Adaptation:
> "something shifted last week and I'm not ready to talk about it yet"
> "I started doing the thing I'd been putting off. Still don't know if it'll work."
> "not posting about the process yet but I'm in it"

---

## 5. Moral Framing Hooks

**What they do:** Pre-load the viewer's emotional interpretation before they see the content. The hook tells them how to feel before the evidence arrives.

---

**Pattern: Named Verdict + Evidence**
> "Day [N] of posting a video where a tech CEO says something diabolically evil in broad daylight"

Why it works: "Diabolically evil" is the verdict. "In broad daylight" is the moral framing. The clip that follows is just proof. The viewer doesn't need to decide how to feel — they've already been told, and they agree or disagree, either way engaging.

Adaptation for your brand (non-political — apply to career/work culture):
> "another example of a workplace that mistakes exhaustion for ambition"
> "a real thing a manager said to someone I know, in a performance review, out loud"
> "this is what passing off emotional labor as 'leadership' looks like"

---

**Pattern: Acknowledged Taboo**
> "dropping wild threats when you're overstimulated AF like"
> "this is everything my mother and I have shoved up our asses"

Why it works: Names something socially uncomfortable, then laughs at it before the audience can judge it. Disarming.

Adaptation:
> "things I've said to myself in a job interview that I should never have said out loud"
> "what I actually thought when they said 'we're like a family here'"
> "the completely unhinged internal monologue I had during my last performance review"

## Hook Writing Checklist

Before using any hook, verify:
- [ ] Could this belong to a generic account? → If yes, make it more specific
- [ ] Does it speak to a feeling the audience already has? → Or does it create one they don't need?
- [ ] Is it completable in 1–2 seconds of reading? → If not, cut it
- [ ] Does it open a loop that the video closes? → Or does it close a loop before the video begins?
- [ ] Does the visual reinforce or contradict the hook? → Should reinforce or intentionally contrast
